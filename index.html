SpaceX Starship Journey

      SpaceX's Starship Test Flight 1, or Starship SN8, aimed to showcase aerodynamic control and Raptor engine performance. 
      
      Launched on December 9, 2020, it successfully reached 12.5 kilometers but experienced a failed landing due to a Raptor engine issue. 
      
      Despite the crash valuable data was gathered for future iterations, advancing SpaceX's goal of a reusable spacecraft for missions to the Moon, Mars, and beyond.

Starship TestFlight - 01

 
             
     SpaceX's Starship Test Flight 2, or Starship SN9, launched on February 2, 2021, with the goal of refining the spacecraft's capabilities. 
    
     Ascending to a similar altitude as its predecessor SN8, SN9 encountered issues during the landing phase. Despite SpaceX's efforts to improve the landing procedure, SN9 experienced a hard landing, leading to an explosion upon impact. 
      
     This outcome highlighted areas for further refinement while contributing valuable insights to the iterative development process of SpaceX's Starship program.

Starship TestFlight - 02

 
        Starship Test Flight 3, or Starship SN10, conducted by SpaceX on March 3, 2021 showcased advancements in the program. 
      
        SN10 achieved a successful ascent demonstrating improved performance compared to its predecessors. However, during the landing phase, a failure in one of the landing legs caused a rapid unscheduled disassembly (RUD). 
     
        Despite the setback, the test provided crucial data for refining future iterations of Starship, marking progress in SpaceX's pursuit of a fully reusable spacecraft for interplanetary missions.

Falcon 9

Total 340 successful launches 

 
    The SpaceX Falcon 9 is a family of partially reusable two-stage-to-orbit launch vehicles designed and manufactured by SpaceX, founded by Elon Musk. 

    It's named after the Millennium Falcon from Star Wars, with the "9" representing the nine engines on its first stage.

    The Falcon 9 has been pivotal in revolutionizing spaceflight by significantly reducing the cost of launching payloads into space through its reusable first stage.
